Consequences of wing coloration for performance and population growth

The plots below allow you to explore how wing coloration influences estimates of performance and population growth for Colias eriphyle in western Colorado. You can examine both past (since 1950) and future (to 2099) conditions. Data are the output from a model described in Buckley and Kingsolver (2019), which combines biophysical and demographic models. You can visualize annual population growth rate (lambda, where 1= stable population and 2= population size doubling), the hours of potential flight activity, egg viability (%), and mean body temperature (°C).

How does the model work? Microclimate models were used to estimate the environmental conditions butterflies experience and then a biophysical model was used to estimate butterfly body temperatures. Egg viability was assumed, based on lab experiments, to decay exponentially as a function of body temperature from 1 at 40°C to 0.75 at 50°C. Potential flight time, based on based temperatures suitable for flight, was used to estimate how many eggs a female butterfly is able to lay since they lay eggs singly on hostplants. Survival and reproduction were combined to predict population growth rates. Daily minimum and maximum air temperatures at 2 m above ground for 1950–2099 were projected from the CMIP5 multimodel ensemble, assuming a mid-range climate warming scenario.
